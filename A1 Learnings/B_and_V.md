# Spanish Study File: The "B" and "V" Merge

## 1. The Core Rule: Identical Sounds
In standard Spanish, **B** and **V** represent the **exact same sound**.    
The English "V" sound (teeth touching the lower lip) does not exist in standard Spanish. 

### Pronunciation Shifts
The sound changes based on **position**, not the letter used:

**Hard B [b]**: Occurs at the beginning of a phrase or after the letters M or N.

    Botar (To throw) — sounds like English "B"
    Enviar (To send) — the "N" sounds like "M," so it's em-byar

**Soft B [β]**: Occurs between vowels. It's a "soft" version where lips almost touch but let air 
through.

    Lava (Lava) — sounds like a breathy, vibrating "B"

## 3. Homophone Practice (Same Sound, Different Meaning)
Memorizing these pairs is key because your ears won't help you tell them apart:

    Vaca    (Cow)                  vs. Baca    (Luggage roof rack)
    Votar   (To vote)              vs. Botar   (To throw away / bounce)
    Bello   (Beautiful)            vs. Vello   (Body hair/fuzz)
    Bienes  (Possessions/Assets)   vs. Vienes  (You come)
    Tubo    (Tube/Pipe)            vs. Tuvo    (He/She had)
    Sabia   (Wise woman)           vs. Savia   (Plant sap)
    Rebelar (To rebel)             vs. Revelar (To reveal)

## 4. Useful Spelling Patterns
While there are exceptions, these patterns help you guess which letter to use:

### Always use B:
- Before the consonants L or R (Blanco, Brazo).
- After the letter M (Cambio, Ambiguo).
- For verb endings like -aba (Hablaba, Cantaba).

### Always use V:
- After the letter N (Enviar, Invierno).
- After the letter D (Advertencia, Adverso).
- For adjectives ending in -ivo/a or -evo/a (Nuevo, Activo).
